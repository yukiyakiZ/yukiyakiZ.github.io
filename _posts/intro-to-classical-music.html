Introduction to Classical Music

January 13, 2019, UTC-08

Finally, I finished the summary notes for the excellent open source Coursera course Introduction to Classical Music that I previously took. Below are the notes I have taken based on the course and the videos contents for future reference (all copyrights belong to the course owners).

Why we like what we like

  • Why music: relax, excite, concentrate ourselves (for work, exercise, leisure); show us a vision of a better world where everything is harmony and balance; bring us all together, reaffirm common values.

    Music is what feelings sound like.

    Victor Hugo
  • Pop vs classical music: Electric instrument vs acoustic instrument generating sound from natural materials; presence of lyrics; strong beat vs subdued beats; pop music typically short, repetitious while classical music can be hours long; pop music often performed without musical notation, different styles could happen to the same piece while classical music is performed with a uniform score.
  • Music selection: e.g., G minor BWV 578 (Bach) makes us think analytically; O mio babbino caro creates a feeling of romance; Chopin's Piano Sonata No 2 in B flat minor, Funeral March brings serenity.
  • How do we hear music? Sound waves and the ear: Neuroscientists have discovered that music has the same effect as many euphoria-inducing stimuli, such as food, sex, and drugs of abuse ...
  • Syntax of western music: language and music work the same way: Music is also about nurture. It is cultural and the syntax expresses the expectation to be fulfilled. Syntax in western music: we expect a leading tone to pull toward the home pitch or tonic; a large leap would be reversed by the following pitch; the harmony must fit the melody; the harmonies usually return home to tonic.
  • Nature except from nurture perspective of music: It applies to all music cultures.
    • Overtone series: When any string on an instrument or column of air in a tube created by a wind instrument, produces a sound, actually it's producing many sounds. Through something of an auditory trick or illusion, we hear only the lowest sound in part because it's the loudest.     • Natural consonance of the overtone series: We could apply the fundamental and overtone series to a keyboard. The overtone series stretches upward across several octaves. If we play these keys together, we will get very consonant sounds. Richard Strauss applies exactly this concept to the beginning of the piece Also sprach Zarathustra.

How Music Works, it's Magic.

  • Beat, meter and rhythm: Rhythm, along with melody, harmony, tone, color, texture and form, are the essential elements of music. Rhythm provides a framework, a framework in time in which sounds, pitches, can rest.
    • Presence of beat (no beat, strong beat, suppressed beat): Some music doesn't have a beat, e.g., a Gregorian chant called Viderunt Omnes. Pop music typically has a strong beat, e.g., Thrift Shop (Macklemore). Classical music typically has a suppressed beat, e.g., Clair de Lune (Debussy), but it could also have a clear beat, e.g., Pomp and Circumstance March (Edward Elgar).
    • Grouping beats: We group the endless flow of beats to make sense of them. Our brain subconsciously group pulses into units of 2 (duple), 3 (triple), or 4. These groupings lead to what we call meter. We have duple meter (strong-weak) and triple meter (strong-weak-weak).
    • 2 axes/coordinates of music: Pitch and duration/sound and time.
  • Musical notation: Western music is the only musical culture that uses musical notation in a controlling way.
    • Duple/triple division: Whole note is divided into two halves, half note into two quarters, quarter note into two eighths, and so on. There's also a triple division, which is affected by means of a dot that adds 50% of the value to the note. E.g., The dotted quarter equal to three eighth notes, not just two. We typically count the quarter note as the main beat, or pulse.
    • Rule of thumb: Higher and lower positions correspond to higher and lower sounds. Lighter or darker notes correspond to slower and faster rhythms.
    • Beat/meter extracted from the complex rhythms - Bolero (Maurice Ravel) example: Three rhythms operating here. One in the flute which carries the melody. One in the snare drum, which is pure rhythm. One in the bass, which provides harmony, which more reveals the information about the beats. These different rhythms are united by a common beat. Our brain digests the complex rhythms and extrapolate the basic structural framework, i.e., the beats/meters. Bolero is written in triple meter.
    • Beat/meter extracted from the complex rhythms - other examples: Largo (Georg Friedrich Händel) is in triple meter; Heidenroslein (Franz Schlubert) is in duple meter; Gymnopodie No.1 (Erik Satie) is in triple meter.
